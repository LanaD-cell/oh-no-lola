Once upon a time,

In a peaceful pond nestled in the heart of a magical forest, there lived a fluffy duckling named Lola. She was kind, curious, and always eager to help others. Today was a big day—her first day of school! But to get there, Lola had to walk through the forest alone for the very first time. With a deep breath and a little quack of determination, Lola waddled into the forest. The sunlight filtered through the tall trees, and birds sang happy songs. Lola smiled but kept her little wings close to her sides. She wasn’t just excited—she was nervous.

Oh, where could it be?

As she waddled along, she heard a frustrated voice behind a bush. “Oh, where could it be? I can’t find it anywhere!” Curious, Lola peeked around the bush and saw a small porcupine pacing in circles. His quills were sticking out in all directions. “Hello! I’m Lola,” she said kindly. “Are you okay?” The porcupine sighed. “I’m Percy. I’ve lost my hat! I was wearing it earlier, but now it’s gone, and I can’t find it!” Lola tilted her head. “When did you last see it?” Percy scratched his head with a quill. “Um… I don’t know. I was walking and looking at flowers, and then it was gone!” Lola thought for a moment. She noticed how flustered Percy looked. “Let’s slow down and check carefully,” she said. Percy nodded, though he kept pacing. “Stop moving for a second, Percy,” Lola said gently. As Percy stood still, Lola waddled closer and gasped. “Percy, your hat is stuck in your quills! It’s been with you this whole time!” Percy’s eyes widened as Lola carefully tugged the hat free. “Oh my quills!” he exclaimed. “How did I not notice?” Lola giggled. “Sometimes, we get so worried that we forget to stop and look carefully. If we’re patient, the answer can be right in front of us.” Percy nodded, blushing. “You’re right, Lola. I need to be more patient. Thank you for helping me!” “You’re welcome, Percy. Enjoy your walk!” Percy waved goodbye, and Lola continued through the forest, feeling happy that she could help.

Can Butter fly!

As she waddled deeper into the forest, Lola spotted a tiny green worm crying on top of a red mushroom. “Hey there, my name is Lola! What’s wrong?” Lola asked, waddling closer. The worm sniffled. “Oh no Lola, I’m Butter and I am terribly sad. I just want to go to normal school, make friends, play and learn. My parents are making me go to flying school. I don’t want to go to flying school! I’m just a worm! How can I possibly fly when I don’t even have wings?” Lola smiled warmly. “Oh Butter, did anyone tell you that one day you’ll be a butterfly?” Butter blinked. “A… butterfly? Me?” Lola nodded. “Yes! One day, you’ll have big, beautiful wings, and you’ll fly all over the place.” Butter looked down at her little worm body. “But I don’t have wings now. What if I never get them?” Lola chuckled. “Wings take time to grow, Butter. But just because you don’t see them now doesn’t mean they’re not coming. Hope is believing in what’s possible, even when you can’t see it yet.” Butter sniffled but smiled. “So… I just have to wait and trust that I’ll be a butterfly one day?” “That’s right,” Lola said. “And when the time comes, your wings will be amazing!” Butter wiggled happily. “Thank you, Lola! I feel much better. I’ll go to flying school and wait for my wings!” “That’s the spirit!” Lola said, waving goodbye as Butter wriggled off.

Be brave

As Lola walked farther, the sunlight began to fade, casting long shadows across the forest floor. Suddenly, she heard a soft whimper. Curious, she followed the sound and found a small fox trapped in a rope snare. Its fluffy tail twitched nervously. “Oh no!” Lola exclaimed, her heart racing. “Are you okay?” The fox looked up with wide, frightened eyes. “Please help me!” it whimpered. Lola hesitated. Her mommy had always warned her to be careful of foxes. “Foxes can be sneaky and tricky,” her mommy had said. “Sometimes, they might even try to hurt you.” But this fox looked so scared and helpless. Lola’s heart tugged at her. “You’re a fox,” she said cautiously. “How do I know you won’t hurt me if I help you?” The fox’s eyes filled with tears. “I know foxes have a bad reputation, but I promise I won’t hurt you. I’m just a baby, and I’m really scared!” Lola took a deep breath. “Okay. I’m going to trust you. Stay still while I help you.” Carefully, Lola found a sharp stone nearby and began to cut the rope. It took time, but finally, the snare loosened, and the fox was free. The fox jumped up, wagging its tail. “Thank you, thank you! You saved me!” “You’re welcome,” Lola said with a smile. “But you need to be more careful. The forest can be dangerous.” The fox nodded. “I will! I know you were scared to help me, but you trusted me. I’ll never forget your kindness.” “Well, sometimes it’s okay to trust, even when you’re afraid,” Lola said. “Just be sure to trust wisely.” The fox beamed. “You’re brave and kind, Lola. Maybe we can be friends?” Lola smiled. “Maybe. Be safe out there!” As Lola continued her journey, she felt proud of all she had learned. Percy taught her the value of patience, Butter reminded her to always have hope, and the fox showed her that trust can overcome fear. By the time she reached school, Lola knew she was ready for anything. With kindness, courage, and a little quack of determination, she waddled into class, excited for the adventures ahead.

School is cool

After a wild trek through the forest, Lola finally saw it—a tiny wooden schoolhouse with flowers everywhere. It looked like something straight out of Duck Pinterest! As she waddled up, she spotted other animals chatting—bunnies, squirrels, and ducklings. They swapped morning adventures like they’d just come back from summer camp. Lola puffed out her feathers. “I walked here alone, helped a porcupine find his hat, a caterpillar figure out flying school, and saved a baby fox from a snare!” she quacked. The teacher, a wise old owl with glasses bigger than her head, beamed. “Impressive, Lola! You’re already overqualified.” Inside, Lola plopped onto a tiny desk, her feet barely touching the floor. The classroom was cozy, with sunbeams, posters, and at least five snacks tucked into squirrel backpacks. The owl kicked off the day with, “The forest is awesome! Every animal’s got a job, even if it’s just not being eaten!” Lola soaked it all up like a sponge. By the end of the day, she had new friends, more stories, and a brain stuffed with forest facts. As she waddled home, Lola quacked to herself, “School’s awesome! Plus, no ropes, no porcupines, just snacks and naps. I could get used to this!”
